# Monte Carlo Simulation of Claim Reserve Adequacy Across Six P&C Lines

Bootstrap chain-ladder simulation of loss reserve adequacy for U.S.
property-casualty insurers, using public NAIC Schedule P data (accident
years 1998–2007) across all six lines of business in the CAS Loss Reserving
Database:

| Line | Companies (cleaned) | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Workers' Compensation | 77 | long-tail |
| Private Passenger Auto | 105 | short-tail |
| Commercial Auto | 109 | medium-tail |
| Medical Malpractice (Claims-Made) | 19 | long-tail |
| Product Liability (Occurrence) | 34 | very long-tail |
| Other Liability (Occurrence) | 170 | very long-tail |

For each insurer, the model projects a full **distribution** of ultimate
losses — not a single point estimate — and compares it against the reserves
the company actually carried, producing a per-company **probability of
reserve shortfall**. Framing reserve adequacy as a probability is directly
relevant to solvency regulation: it is the quantity state insurance
departments and the NAIC care about when assessing whether carried reserves
are sufficient. Running all six Schedule P lines through one pipeline also
allows a like-for-like comparison of reserve risk between short-tail and
long-tail business.

## Data

Source: [Loss Reserving Data Pulled from NAIC Schedule P with Additional
Accident Years](https://www.casact.org/publications-research/research/research-resources/loss-reserving-data-pulled-naic-schedule-p),
Casualty Actuarial Society (CAS). The current data (December 2025) was
compiled for the CAS by **S&P Global Market Intelligence**; this project
acknowledges accessing the files from the CAS research page as compiled by
S&P, per the CAS's request.

The data comes from **NAIC Schedule P**, filed by every U.S.
property-casualty insurer, covering six lines: private passenger auto,
commercial auto, workers' compensation, medical malpractice (claims-made),
other liability (occurrence), and product liability (occurrence). Each row
is one (company, accident year, development lag) snapshot — 10 accident
years (1998–2007) x 10 development lags per company, net of reinsurance.

Key columns used here: `CumPaidLoss` (cumulative paid losses),
`EarnedPremNet` (net earned premium), and `PostedReserves2007` (reserves
posted at year-end 2007 — the adequacy benchmark). `GRCODE`/`GRNAME`
identify the insurer (group or single entity, per the `Single` flag).

CAS/S&P also applied their own sampling filter before publishing (complete
10x10 history, consistent reporting across years, non-zero premium) — this
project's own cleaning (`src/clean_data.py`) applies a similar filter on
top of that. See `data/raw/SOURCE.md` for the file-to-line mapping.

## Method

**Bootstrap chain ladder** (non-parametric). Not ELR or Bornhuetter-Ferguson:
no premium-based prior expected losses are used anywhere.

1. **Clean** (`src/clean_data.py`) — per line, drop companies with
   incomplete triangles, non-positive 2007 premium, or non-positive
   paid-to-date. Every exclusion is logged with its reason in
   `data/processed/{line}_dropped_companies.csv`.
2. **Triangle** (`src/build_triangle.py`) — reshape each company's rows
   into a 10×10 cumulative-paid-loss matrix, masking cells not yet
   observable as of year-end 2007. Negative cumulative paid losses (a real
   Schedule P phenomenon driven by subrogation, salvage, reinsurance
   recoveries, voided payments, and deductible collections) are floored at
   zero, per standard actuarial practice for chain-ladder methods.
3. **Link ratios** (`src/link_ratios.py`) — for each development step,
   collect the empirical per-accident-year link ratios. These pools, not a
   single averaged factor, are what the simulation draws from.
4. **Simulate** (`src/simulate.py`) — 5,000 bootstrap iterations per
   company: resample one link ratio per development step, project every
   accident year to full development, sum to a total ultimate loss.
   Vectorized with NumPy (all six lines, 514 companies, run in ~20s).
5. **Aggregate** (`src/run_pipeline.py`) — run any or all lines; output
   per-line results plus a combined cross-line results table.
6. **Figures** (`src/make_figures.py`) — a single-company end-to-end visual
   per line, plus the cross-line risk comparison.

All parameters (accident-year window, simulation count, seed, file paths)
live in `src/config.py`.

## Reproducing

```bash
pip install -r requirements.txt

python src/clean_data.py all      # data/processed/*.csv
python src/run_pipeline.py all    # results/*.csv  (~20 seconds)
python src/make_figures.py all    # results/figures/*.png
```

Any step also runs for a single line (`python src/run_pipeline.py medmal`),
and the triangle / link-ratio / simulate modules each run standalone on an
example company for inspection (`python src/build_triangle.py othliab`).
Results are deterministic (fixed random seed).

## Headline results

| Line | Mean P(shortfall) | Companies P>50% | Mean CV of ultimate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Other Liability | 24.1% | 18.8% | 0.455 |
| Product Liability | 31.8% | 35.3% | 0.454 |
| Medical Malpractice | 18.5% | 15.8% | 0.343 |
| Commercial Auto | 26.9% | 18.4% | 0.139 |
| Workers' Compensation | 12.9% | 11.7% | 0.097 |
| Private Passenger Auto | 22.9% | 18.1% | 0.031 |

Two observations worth noting:

- **Reserve uncertainty tracks tail length almost perfectly.** The
  coefficient of variation of simulated ultimates sorts by line exactly as
  actuarial theory predicts: occurrence liability lines (~45%) > medical
  malpractice (~34%) > commercial auto (~14%) > workers' comp (~10%) >
  private passenger auto (~3%).
- **Shortfall probability does not.** Private passenger auto — the most
  predictable line — shows a *higher* mean shortfall probability (22.9%)
  than workers' compensation (12.9%). Predictability and reserve margin are
  different things: long-tail lines appear to be reserved with more
  cushion relative to their uncertainty, while short-tail reserves are set
  tighter to expected values.

## Limitations

- Link ratios are resampled independently across development steps; serial
  correlation in development is not modeled.
- Tail development beyond lag 10 is not modeled (losses are treated as
  fully developed at lag 10) — a meaningful simplification for the
  occurrence liability lines.
- Company-level triangles only; no industry credibility weighting for
  small, noisy books. Medical malpractice (19 companies) is a thin sample.
- `PostedReserves2007` is an all-lines company figure in these files, so
  the adequacy comparison is approximate for multi-line insurers.
